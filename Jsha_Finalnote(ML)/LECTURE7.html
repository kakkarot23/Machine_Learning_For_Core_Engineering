Topic: Population Statistics (Mean, Variance, Covariance, Correlation)

üìù One-Liner Notes

Population vs. Sample

  • The **Population** is the entire set of data points of interest.
  • The **Sample** is a representative subset of the population used for statistical analysis.
  • Statistical properties of the population can be inferred from its **underlying probability density function (PDF)**.

Moments and Measures

  • The **Population Mean ($\mu$)**, or **Expected Value ($E[X]$)**, is the **first moment** of the PDF.
  • The **Population Variance ($\sigma^2$)** quantifies the extent of **spread** in a distribution.
  • The Variance is calculated as the expected value of the square of the difference between $X$ and $\mu$.
  • The **Standard Deviation ($\sigma$)** is the square root of the variance and has the **same unit** as the random variable.
  • The **Covariance ($Cov(X, Y)$)** quantifies the degree of **linear correlation** between two random variables.
  • The **Correlation Coefficient ($\rho_{XY}$)** is the **normalized measure** of the correlation between two random variables.

‚úÖ Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

**MCQ 1: The population mean $E[X]$ is mathematically defined as the:**

A. Second moment of the PDF $f(x)$ B. Integral of $x^2 f(x) dx$ over the domain C. First moment of the PDF $f(x)$ D. Square root of the variance
Answer: C. The population mean (or expected value) $E[X]$ is calculated as $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x f(x) dx$, which is the definition of the **first moment** of the PDF.

**MCQ 2: What is the relationship between $\text{Cov}(X, Y)$ and the variables $X$ and $Y$ when $\text{Cov}(X, Y) > 0$?**

A. $X$ and $Y$ are independent. B. Larger values of $X$ correspond to lower values of $Y$. C. Larger values of $X$ correspond to larger values of $Y$. D. $X$ and $Y$ are anti-correlated.
Answer: C. When $\text{Cov}(X, Y) > 0$, it means that larger values of $X$ tend to correspond to larger values of $Y$ (positive correlation).

**MCQ 3: The Correlation Coefficient $\rho_{XY}$ is a normalized measure, calculated by dividing the Covariance by:**

A. The product of the means $\mu_X \mu_Y$ B. The sum of the variances $\sigma_X^2 + \sigma_Y^2$ C. The product of the standard deviations $\sigma_X \sigma_Y$ D. The square of the Expected Value $E[XY]^2$
Answer: C. The Pearson's correlation coefficient is the covariance normalized by the product of the standard deviations: $\rho_{XY} = \frac{\text{Cov}(X, Y)}{\sigma_X \sigma_Y}$.

‚ùì Short Question & Answer

**Q1: Write the mathematical expression for Population Variance $\text{Var}(X)$ in terms of Expected Values.**

**A:** The Population Variance is defined as: $$\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2$$ where $E[X^2]$ is the second moment of the PDF.

**Q2: What is the significance of the two different outcomes of $\text{Cov}(X, Y) < 0$ and $\text{Cov}(X, Y) \approx 0$?**

**A:**

  • $\text{Cov}(X, Y) < 0$ implies **negative correlation**: larger values of $X$ correspond to lower values of $Y$.
  • $\text{Cov}(X, Y) \approx 0$ implies **no linear correlation**: $X$ and $Y$ are either independent or have a non-linear relationship.

**Q3: How does the Standard Deviation $\sigma$ relate to the Variance $\sigma^2$ in terms of units?**

**A:** The Standard Deviation ($\sigma$) is the square root of the variance ($\sigma^2$). This operation ensures that the standard deviation has the **same unit** as the random variable $X$ itself, making it more interpretable than variance in the physical world.

üìö Long Q&A / Description / Graphical Questions

**Q1: Define and provide the formulas for the Population Mean, Variance, and Covariance, explaining what each statistical property quantifies.**

**A: Key Statistical Properties of a Population (from the PDF):**

Population Mean / Expected Value ($\mu$ or $E[X]$):
Quantifies the **central tendency** of the distribution. It is the average value that the random variable $X$ is expected to take over the long run. $$\mu = E[X] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x f(x) dx$$
Population Variance ($\sigma^2$ or $\text{Var}(X)$):
Quantifies the **extent of spread or dispersion** of the random variable around its mean. $$\text{Var}(X) = E[(X-\mu)^2] = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2$$
Covariance ($\text{Cov}(X, Y)$):
Quantifies the **degree of linear correlation** between two random variables $X$ and $Y$. $$\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[XY] - E[X] E[Y]$$

**Q2: Prove that the Covariance of two independent random variables $X$ and $Y$ is zero, assuming their joint PDF is $f_{XY}(x, y) = f_X(x) f_Y(y)$.**

**A: Proof:**

  1. **Covariance Definition:** The covariance of $X$ and $Y$ is defined as: $$\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[XY] - E[X] E[Y]$$
  2. **Expected Value $E[XY]$ for Independent Variables:** The expected value $E[XY]$ is calculated using the Joint PDF, $f_{XY}(x, y)$: $$E[XY] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} xy f_{XY}(x, y) dx dy$$
  3. **Substitute for Independence:** For independent random variables, the Joint PDF is the product of the marginal PDFs: $f_{XY}(x, y) = f_X(x) f_Y(y)$. $$E[XY] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} xy f_X(x) f_Y(y) dx dy$$
  4. **Separate the Integrals:** Since the variables are separable, the integral can be factored: $$E[XY] = \left( \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x f_X(x) dx \right) \left( \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} y f_Y(y) dy \right)$$
  5. **Identify Expected Values:** Recognizing the definition of $E[X]$ and $E[Y]$: $$E[X] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x f_X(x) dx$$ $$E[Y] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} y f_Y(y) dy$$ Therefore, $E[XY] = E[X] E[Y]$.
  6. **Final Substitution into Covariance Formula:** $$\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[XY] - E[X] E[Y] = E[X] E[Y] - E[X] E[Y] = 0$$

**Conclusion:** For independent random variables, the covariance is always **zero** ($\text{Cov}(X, Y) = 0$).
